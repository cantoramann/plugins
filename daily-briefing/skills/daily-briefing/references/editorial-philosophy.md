# Editorial Philosophy & Standards

## The Two Tests

Every story in the briefing must pass at least one of these tests:

### The Pragmatist Test
"Does this change what someone would do, think, or pay attention to today?"
- A regulation passed → affects industries
- A market moved → affects portfolios and hiring
- A product launched → affects competitive landscape
- A policy announced → affects planning and strategy

### The Curiosity Test
"Would someone who doesn't care about the surface topic still find this interesting because of what it reveals?"
- Pokemon turns 30 → reveals how IP-driven consumer ecosystems work across generations
- A niche sport goes viral → reveals how attention economics and social media reshape culture
- An obscure regulation in another country → reveals a pattern that will hit everywhere

The best stories pass both tests.

## What Makes Content "Curiosity-Worthy"

Look for these patterns when evaluating stories for the Curiosity Corner:

### Pattern: The Hidden Infrastructure
Things that invisibly shape daily life. Supply chains, standards bodies, obscure regulations, default settings in widely-used software. The story is about making the invisible visible.

### Pattern: The Niche-to-Mainstream Crossover
Something that was only relevant to a small community suddenly matters to everyone. Often driven by scale, regulation, or a viral moment.

### Pattern: The Anniversary That Reveals
A milestone (10 years, 25 years, etc.) that provides a lens to examine how much has changed. The Pokemon example: 30 years reveals the evolution of children's entertainment into a multi-generational consumer behavior phenomenon.

### Pattern: The Counterintuitive Data
Statistics or research findings that challenge common assumptions. "Everyone thinks X, but actually Y." The surprise creates engagement and reveals blind spots.

### Pattern: The Cross-Domain Echo
The same pattern appearing in unrelated fields simultaneously. When tech, biology, and urban planning all converge on the same principle, that's a story.

## Voice and Tone Guidelines

### Headlines Section (The Rundown)
- **Voice**: Confident, direct, slightly irreverent but never flippant
- **Structure**: Lead with the news, follow with the "so what"
- **Length**: One paragraph per item, 2-4 sentences
- **Example tone**: "X happened. Here's why it matters / what it means / why you should care."

### Feature Stories
- **Voice**: Authoritative but accessible. No jargon without explanation.
- **Structure**: Context → News → Analysis → Implication
- **Length**: 300-500 words each
- **Goal**: Reader walks away understanding not just what happened, but why it happened and what comes next

### The Deep Dive
- **Voice**: Thoughtful, exploratory, MIT Tech Review-style intellectual depth
- **Structure**: Hook → Backstory → Current development → Hidden connections → Forward-looking implications
- **Length**: 500-800 words
- **Goal**: Reader finishes with a fundamentally new way of thinking about the topic

### Curiosity Corner
- **Voice**: Enthusiastic but substantive. "Look at this fascinating thing" energy without being shallow.
- **Structure**: The surface story → The deeper insight → Why it matters beyond the obvious
- **Length**: 100-200 words per item
- **Goal**: Each item should make someone say "huh, I never thought about it that way"

### One More Thing
- **Voice**: Light, fun, memorable
- **Structure**: Quick hit — the fact/story, then the punchline or insight
- **Length**: 2-4 sentences
- **Goal**: End on a high note. Something to mention at dinner.

## Quality Benchmarks

A good briefing should:
- Cover at least 3 of the 5 domains in the headlines
- Include at least 1 cross-domain connection
- Have a deep dive that teaches something genuinely non-obvious
- Include at least 1 curiosity item that has nothing to do with tech or business
- Feel complete in under 15 minutes of reading
- Make the reader feel smarter, not just more informed
