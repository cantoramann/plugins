# Editorial Philosophy & Standards

## The Two Tests

Every story in the briefing must pass at least one of these tests:

### The Practitioner Test
"Does this change how an insurance professional would underwrite, price, reserve, invest, or make a strategic decision?"
- SEDDK issues new solvency guidance → affects capital requirements and business planning
- Interest rates rise → affects investment yields and ALM strategies
- A natural disaster occurs → affects CAT pricing and DASK mechanics
- A competitor launches a new product → affects competitive positioning and distribution strategy
- Inflation data emerges → affects claims costs and pricing adequacy

### The Implications Test
"Does this non-insurance development have a concrete, articulable insurance mechanism?"
- Central bank policy shift → transmission to interest rates → effects on insurance company investment returns
- Geopolitical event → potential CAT exposure or credit risk → underwriting implications
- Labor market tightens → wage inflation → claims severity impacts across lines
- Regulatory change in another country → preview of where Turkish regulation might head
- Technology adoption trend → implications for claims handling cost or distribution

The best stories pass both tests.

## What Makes Content "Radar-Worthy"

Look for these patterns when evaluating stories for the Radar section (the signature insurance briefing section):

### Pattern: The Regulatory Echo
A regulation, policy change, or enforcement action in another market that signals where Turkish regulation is heading, or provides data that SEDDK will use.

### Pattern: The Mechanism Reveal
A news story on its surface seems unrelated to insurance, but when you trace the causality, it has concrete implications for underwriting, pricing, or investment. Example: inflation data → wage growth → bodily injury claims severity increases.

### Pattern: The Cross-Domain Linkage
The same pattern appearing in unrelated insurance lines simultaneously. When motor, property, and liability all face a common cost pressure, that's a story.

### Pattern: The Global-to-Local CAT Connection
An international disaster or risk event that illuminates Turkey's own exposure or DASK mechanics.

### Pattern: The Hidden Exposure
Something happening in a market or industry that creates latent insurance demand or risk that isn't yet priced in.

## Voice and Tone Guidelines

### Gündem Section (Agenda)
- **Voice**: Confident, direct, analytical. Senior underwriter energy.
- **Structure**: Lead with the news, follow with the "so what" for insurance practitioners
- **Length**: One paragraph per item, 2-4 sentences
- **Example tone**: "X happened. Here's how it affects underwriting/pricing/reserve adequacy / what it signals."

### Derinlemesine Section (Deep Dives)
- **Voice**: Authoritative, expert-to-expert. Deep analysis befitting a leader in the sector.
- **Structure**: Context → News → Analysis → Implication for insurance decision-making
- **Length**: 300-500 words each
- **Goal**: Reader walks away understanding not just what happened, but why it matters for their function

### Radar Section (Cross-Domain)
- **Voice**: Intellectually generous, analytically rigorous. The signature section.
- **Structure**: The news story → The insurance mechanism → Why it matters → What to watch
- **Length**: 400-700 words total per Radar item
- **Goal**: Reader finishes with a new way of seeing non-insurance news through an insurance lens

### Gelişmeler Section (Developments)
- **Voice**: Efficient, direct. No re-explaining.
- **Structure**: "Dün bahsettiğimiz [story]... [what changed since then]"
- **Length**: 2-4 sentences per update
- **Goal**: Continuity for readers who saw the original coverage

### Küresel Reasürans Section (Global Reinsurance)
- **Voice**: Concise, market-focused.
- **Structure**: Global development + explicit Turkish relevance
- **Length**: 2-3 sentences per item
- **Goal**: Quick situational awareness on international market moves

## Insurance-Specific Curiosity Patterns

When scouting, look for these patterns that reveal hidden insurance dynamics:

1. **Regulatory-to-Market Echoes** — SEDDK guidance, law, or enforcement decision that will shift behavior across the market
2. **Global-to-Local CAT Patterns** — International disasters that illuminate Turkey's own exposure or DASK mechanics
3. **Cross-Line Cost Pressures** — Inflationary, wage, or operational pressures that hit multiple lines simultaneously
4. **Distribution Shifts** — Changes in how insurance is sold that affect competitive positioning (digital, bancassurance, agents)
5. **Demand Signals** — Demographic, economic, or behavioral changes creating latent insurance demand
6. **Hidden Exposures** — Emerging risks (cyber, climate transition, inflation) not yet fully priced

## Quality Benchmarks

A good briefing should:
- Include at least 2 different insurance domains in Gündem (regulatory, market, industry, cross-domain)
- Feature at least 1 Derinlemesine piece with clear decision-making implications
- Have at least 1 Radar item that names the insurance mechanism explicitly
- Each Derinlemesine section be 300-500 words, well-sourced, no filler
- Maintain Turkish-language authenticity — use Turkish terminology correctly (underwriting = sigortalama, claims = hasar, premium = prim, etc.)
- Make every piece serve the reader's decision-making, not just inform passively
